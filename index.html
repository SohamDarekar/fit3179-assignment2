Australia's Biodiversity Crisis · 2026

A Nation's Wildlife On the Edge

Australia is one of Earth's 17 megadiverse nations - home to over one million species, most of which exist nowhere else on Earth. Yet its record of species loss is among the worst in the developed world. This visualisation explores the scale, geography, and trajectory of Australia's threatened species crisis.

Section 01 - The Big Picture

2,208 species facing extinction - and counting

Under Australia's Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (EPBC Act), 2,208 species are formally listed as threatened. Plants make up the largest share, but animals - from birds and mammals to sharks and frogs - are disproportionately at risk. This section breaks down who is most at risk and why.

Section 02 - Geography of Crisis

Where Australia's threatened species live

Threatened species are not distributed evenly across the continent. States with high biodiversity and habitat diversity - Queensland, New South Wales, and Western Australia - also carry the greatest threatened species burden. Some states are home to species found nowhere else on Earth, making local habitat protection critical.

Map 1 - Total Threatened Species by State & Territory
A choropleth map of Australia showing total EPBC-listed threatened species per state. Darker shades indicate a higher species burden.
New South Wales leads with 760 threatened species, followed closely by Queensland (612) and Western Australia (614). The ACT, despite its small size, still harbours 71 threatened species - many endemic to the alpine region.
Threatened Species by State, Broken Down by Status
Each bar shows the composition of threatened listings across the four major threat categories for each state and territory.
Geographically Isolated: Single-State Endemics
Species listed in only one state are particularly vulnerable to localised threats. Queensland and NSW have the highest number of these geographically isolated threatened species.
Map 2 - Wildlife Monitoring Sites Across Australia
Each point represents a wildlife monitoring location from the Australian Threatened Species Index. Colour indicates species threat status at that site. Hover for details.
Monitoring sites are densely clustered along the eastern and south-western coastlines - reflecting both biodiversity hotspots and the concentration of human research infrastructure. Remote inland areas, where many species also live, are comparatively under-monitored.
Section 03 - Birds in Crisis

Australia's threatened birds: a monitoring story

The Australian Threatened Species Index tracks population trends for birds and mammals across thousands of monitoring sites. For birds - Australia's most-monitored group of threatened fauna - the data reveals a deeply concerning long-term decline in population indices, particularly for Critically Endangered species.

Section 04 - Plants & Families

Australia's silent crisis: threatened plants

Australia's threatened flora crisis receives far less public attention than its iconic fauna. Yet 1,514 plant species are formally listed - nearly 69% of all threatened species. Orchids, eucalypts, wattles, and banksias are among the most affected plant families, driven by land clearing, invasive species, altered fire regimes, and climate change.

The 12 plant families with the greatest number of EPBC-listed threatened species, broken down by Critically Endangered, Endangered, and Vulnerable categories.
Threatened Plant Species by Category
A breakdown of Australia's 1,514 threatened plant species by EPBC status. More than 20% are Critically Endangered - facing the most immediate risk of extinction.
Habitat Types and Threat Severity
A heatmap showing the number of monitored threatened species by habitat type and EPBC threat category. Terrestrial and arboreal habitats dominate, but coastal and wetland species face particularly acute risks relative to their small numbers.
Section 05 - Looking Forward

What the data tells us - and what we must do

The patterns in the data are clear: species loss is geographically concentrated, taxonomically skewed, and accelerating. Understanding which states carry the most species, which families are most at risk, and where monitoring is weakest are critical first steps toward action.

Species Richness vs. Endemic Vulnerability by State
States with more total threatened species also tend to have more geographically isolated (single-state) species. Each point is a state - size reflects the percentage of species found only in that state.
Shared Threatened Species Between State Pairs
A dot matrix showing how many threatened species are shared between pairs of Australian states. High overlap suggests interconnected threats requiring coordinated conservation.
Mammal Population Monitoring Index Across Monitoring Sites (1990–2022)
Average abundance index for monitored threatened mammals across all Australian sites. Unlike birds, mammal monitoring coverage is sparser but shows a deeply worrying downward trajectory, particularly since 2010.
Combined Monitoring Trends: Birds vs. Mammals vs. Amphibians (1990–2022)
All three vertebrate groups show an average population monitoring index trending below their 1990 baseline, with Amphibians showing the steepest and most erratic decline - driven by the chytrid fungus pandemic.
