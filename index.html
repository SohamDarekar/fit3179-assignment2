Australia's Biodiversity Crisis · 2026

A Nation's Wildlife On the Edge

Australia is one of Earth's 17 megadiverse nations - home to over one million species, most of which exist nowhere else on Earth. Yet its record of species loss is among the worst in the developed world. This visualisation explores the scale, geography, and trajectory of Australia's threatened species crisis.

Section 01 - The Big Picture

2,208 species facing extinction - and counting

Under Australia's Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (EPBC Act), 2,208 species are formally listed as threatened. Plants make up the largest share, but animals - from birds and mammals to sharks and frogs - are disproportionately at risk. This section breaks down who is most at risk and why.

Section 02 - Geography of Crisis

Where Australia's threatened species live

Threatened species are not distributed evenly across the continent. States with high biodiversity and habitat diversity, notably Queensland, New South Wales, and Western Australia, carry the greatest threatened species burden. Some states are home to species found nowhere else on Earth, making local habitat protection critical. Understanding where species are concentrated reveals where conservation action is most urgent.

Section 03 - Birds in Crisis

Australia's threatened birds: a monitoring story

The Australian Threatened Species Index tracks population trends for birds and mammals across thousands of monitoring sites. For birds - Australia's most-monitored group of threatened fauna - the data reveals a deeply concerning long-term decline in population indices, particularly for Critically Endangered species.

Population Index Trends for Threatened Birds (1990–2022)
Population monitoring index (1990 = 100) for Australian threatened bird species, split by EPBC threat category. Critically Endangered birds have lost over 90% of their 1990 population. The 2019–20 megafires accelerated the decline for already-stressed species.
Chart 6 of 14
By 2022, Critically Endangered birds had fallen to just 7.8% of their 1990 baseline: a 92% collapse over 32 years. The 2019–20 megafires caused the steepest single-year drop recorded.

Section 04 - Plants & Families

Australia's silent crisis: threatened plants

Australia's threatened flora crisis receives far less public attention than its iconic fauna. Yet 1,514 plant species are formally listed - nearly 69% of all threatened species. Orchids, eucalypts, wattles, and banksias are among the most affected plant families, driven by land clearing, invasive species, altered fire regimes, and climate change.

Section 05 - Looking Forward

What the data tells us - and what we must do

The patterns in the data are clear: species loss is geographically concentrated, taxonomically skewed, and accelerating. Understanding which states carry the most species, which families are most at risk, and where monitoring is weakest are critical first steps toward action.

Dumbbell chart comparing total threatened species (red dot) against endemic threatened species (orange dot) per state. The gap between dots reveals how many species are shared with other states; wider gaps indicate greater cross-border conservation need.

Chord diagram showing interstate ecological overlap. Ribbon thickness represents shared threatened species between each pair of states. Hover a ribbon to see exact counts; hover a state arc to highlight all its connections. NSW–QLD (224) and NSW–VIC (202) dominate.

Mammal Population Monitoring Index Across Monitoring Sites (1990–2022)
Average abundance index for monitored threatened mammals across all Australian sites. Unlike birds, mammal monitoring coverage is sparser but shows a deeply worrying downward trajectory, particularly since 2010.
Chart 13 of 14
Threatened mammal populations have declined by an estimated 77% since 1990. The rate of decline accelerated sharply after 2010, coinciding with feral predator range expansions and intensifying drought cycles.
Combined Monitoring Trends: Birds vs. Mammals vs. Amphibians (1990–2022)
All three vertebrate groups show an average population monitoring index trending below their 1990 baseline, with Amphibians showing the steepest and most erratic decline - driven by the chytrid fungus pandemic.
Chart 14 of 14
Amphibians show the steepest collapse, down to ~15% of 1990 levels, driven by the Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (chytrid) fungus pandemic, which has caused more vertebrate extinctions than any other disease in recorded history. All three groups show no sign of recovery.
